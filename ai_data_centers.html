Residents Say They Want Answers Before More AI Data Centers Are Built

By Staff Reporter •

Communities across the United States are increasingly demanding more information about proposed artificial intelligence data center projects, raising concerns about water use, electricity demand, noise, and the way some developments are being approved before residents fully understand their potential impact.

The issue has gained national attention through a new reporting initiative launched by consumer advocate Erin Brockovich, whose organization says it has collected thousands of complaints and concerns from residents living near existing or proposed data center developments.

According to Brockovich's campaign, nearly 4,000 submissions were received within the first month of the effort, with residents from dozens of states reporting concerns ranging from utility costs and environmental impacts to what many describe as a lack of transparency in the planning process.

"The single most common concern — more than noise, more than water usage, more than rising utility bills — is the one word that keeps appearing in submission after submission: transparency."

— Erin Brockovich

Why Communities Are Raising Concerns

The campaign argues that many communities are not learning about large-scale data center projects until key approvals have already been secured. Brockovich has emphasized that her effort is not intended to oppose artificial intelligence or data center development outright.

"I'm not making a blanket argument against data centers."

"...projects announced after permits are already secured, developers who don't return calls, local officials who signed NDAs before their neighbors knew a project was being considered."

— Erin Brockovich

According to reporting by TechCrunch, Business Insider, and Tom's Hardware, residents participating in the campaign frequently cite concerns about water consumption, electricity demand, utility rates, traffic, noise, and the use of non-disclosure agreements during negotiations between developers and local governments.

Why AI Is Driving More Data Center Construction

The concerns come as technology companies rapidly expand infrastructure needed to support artificial intelligence systems. AI applications require substantial computing power, driving demand for new data centers across the country.

Industry groups and technology companies argue that the facilities are essential for supporting cloud services, AI development, economic growth, and future digital infrastructure.

How Companies Are Responding

Technology companies have acknowledged growing public scrutiny surrounding new projects. Microsoft announced earlier this year that it would discontinue the use of non-disclosure agreements during certain early-stage data center discussions with local governments.

The company also introduced what it calls a "Community-First AI Infrastructure Plan," which includes commitments related to community engagement, energy efficiency, and water stewardship.

Meanwhile, public officials in several states have begun examining how large AI-related infrastructure projects should be reviewed. In Utah, lawmakers and regulators have explored frameworks intended to address concerns involving water resources, electricity demand, air quality, and quality-of-life impacts.

What Happens Next

Developers generally maintain that projects undergo existing regulatory review processes and can provide jobs, investment, and tax revenue to host communities. Community advocates, however, argue that residents often want more information earlier in the process so they can better understand potential tradeoffs before decisions are finalized.

As AI infrastructure investment continues accelerating, the debate increasingly appears focused on transparency and local oversight rather than opposition to artificial intelligence itself.

Additional communities may seek greater disclosure requirements, while companies and policymakers face growing pressure to explain how future projects could affect local resources, public services, and long-term planning decisions.
