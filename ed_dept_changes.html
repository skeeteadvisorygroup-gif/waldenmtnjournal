Millions of Students Could Be Affected by the Latest Education Department Changes

By Staff Reporter •

Millions of students, parents, educators, and school districts could be affected by a major restructuring of federal education oversight announced by the Trump administration, which plans to transfer responsibility for special education programs to the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) and move education-related civil rights enforcement responsibilities to the Department of Justice (DOJ).

According to reporting from Reuters and the Associated Press, the changes are part of a broader effort to reduce the size and responsibilities of the Department of Education while shifting certain functions to other federal agencies.

What Is Changing?

Under the announced restructuring, HHS will oversee federal special education functions currently housed within the Department of Education, while DOJ will assume responsibility for enforcing federal civil rights protections in education.

The Office of Special Education and Rehabilitative Services (OSERS) currently administers programs supporting students with disabilities and helps oversee implementation of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), the federal law guaranteeing eligible students access to a free and appropriate public education.

The Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights currently investigates complaints involving alleged discrimination based on race, sex, disability, religion, and other protected categories in schools and colleges receiving federal funding.

"Through our partnership with HHS, we will align federal services with the goal of strengthening academic outcomes and supporting individuals with disabilities."

— Education Secretary Linda McMahon

Why the Administration Says It Is Making the Change

Administration officials have framed the move as an effort to streamline federal operations and reduce duplication across agencies.

According to Reuters, Secretary McMahon has repeatedly described her mission as reducing bureaucracy within the Department of Education while preserving core services through other federal departments.

Supporters argue that HHS already administers numerous disability-related programs, while DOJ has longstanding experience enforcing federal civil rights laws.

How Students, Parents, and Schools Could Be Affected

For most families, there are currently no immediate changes to special education rights, federal funding programs, or civil rights protections.

IDEA remains federal law, and existing anti-discrimination protections remain in effect regardless of which agency administers them.

However, education policy experts say schools, state agencies, and advocacy organizations will be closely watching how responsibilities are transferred and whether complaint processing, regulatory guidance, or oversight practices change during implementation.

Responses From Advocacy Groups

Disability-rights organizations have expressed concern that moving special education oversight away from an education-focused agency could create uncertainty for families and educators navigating federal requirements.

Civil rights advocates have similarly raised questions about how education-related discrimination complaints will be handled under DOJ oversight and whether enforcement priorities or response times could change.

Advocacy groups emphasize that much will depend on how the transition is implemented and whether agencies provide clear guidance to schools and families.

What Happens Next?

The administration has announced the transfers, but implementation details are still expected to be developed through interagency agreements, guidance documents, and administrative actions.

Schools, parents, educators, and state officials are expected to monitor upcoming announcements from the Department of Education, HHS, and DOJ regarding timelines, staffing, complaint procedures, and oversight responsibilities.

Observers will also be watching for potential congressional responses, legal challenges, and additional agency guidance as the restructuring moves forward.
